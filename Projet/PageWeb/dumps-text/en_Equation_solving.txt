   In [113]mathematics, to solve an equation is to find its solutions,
   which are the values ([114]numbers, [115]functions, [116]sets, etc.)
   that fulfill the condition stated by the [117]equation, consisting
   generally of two [118]expressions related by an [119]equals sign. When
   seeking a solution, one or more [120]variables are designated as
   [121]unknowns. A solution is an assignment of values to the unknown
   variables that makes the equality in the equation true. In other words,
   a solution is a value or a collection of values (one for each unknown)
   such that, when [122]substituted for the unknowns, the equation becomes
   an [123]equality. A solution of an equation is often called a root of
   the equation, particularly but not only for [124]polynomial equations.
   The set of all solutions of an equation is its [125]solution set.

   An equation may be solved either [126]numerically or symbolically.
   Solving an equation numerically means that only numbers are admitted as
   solutions. Solving an equation symbolically means that expressions can
   be used for representing the solutions.

   For example, the equation x + y = 2x - 1 is solved for the unknown x by
   the expression x = y + 1, because substituting y + 1 for x in the
   equation results in (y + 1) + y = 2(y + 1) - 1, a true statement. It is
   also possible to take the variable y to be the unknown, and then the
   equation is solved by y = x - 1. Or x and y can both be treated as
   unknowns, and then there are many solutions to the equation; a symbolic
   solution is (x, y) = (a + 1, a), where the variable a may take any
   value. Instantiating a symbolic solution with specific numbers gives a
   numerical solution; for example, a = 0 gives (x, y) = (1, 0) (that is,
   x = 1, y = 0), and a = 1 gives (x, y) = (2, 1).

   The distinction between known variables and unknown variables is
   generally made in the statement of the problem, by phrases such as "an
   equation in x and y", or "solve for x and y", which indicate the
   unknowns, here x and y. However, it is common to reserve x, y, z, ...
   to denote the unknowns, and to use a, b, c, ... to denote the known
   variables, which are often called [127]parameters. This is typically
   the case when considering [128]polynomial equations, such as
   [129]quadratic equations. However, for some problems, all variables may
   assume either role.

   Depending on the context, solving an equation may consist to find
   either any solution (finding a single solution is enough), all
   solutions, or a solution that satisfies further properties, such as
   belonging to a given [130]interval. When the task is to find the
   solution that is the best under some criterion, this is an
   [131]optimization problem. Solving an optimization problem is generally
   not referred to as "equation solving", as, generally, solving methods
   start from a particular solution for finding a better solution, and
   repeating the process until finding eventually the best solution.

Solution sets

   [[143]edit]
   [144][220px-Ellipse_in_coordinate_system_with_semi-axes_labelled.svg.pn
   g] The solution set of the equation &#8288;x^2/4&#8288; + y^2 = 1 forms
   an [145]ellipse when interpreted as a set of [146]Cartesian coordinate
   pairs.
   Main article: [147]Solution set

   The [148]solution set of a given set of equations or [149]inequalities
   is the [150]set of all its solutions, a solution being a [151]tuple of
   values, one for each [152]unknown, that satisfies all the equations or
   inequalities. If the [153]solution set is empty, then there are no
   values of the unknowns that satisfy simultaneously all equations and
   inequalities.

   For a simple example, consider the equation

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
          displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow
          class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>2</mn> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo>
          <mn>2.</mn> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
          encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
          x^{2}=2.}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
          {\displaystyle x^{2}=2.}

   This equation can be viewed as a [154]Diophantine equation, that is, an
   equation for which only [155]integer solutions are sought. In this
   case, the solution set is the [156]empty set, since 2 is not the
   [157]square of an integer. However, if one searches for [158]real
   solutions, there are two solutions, SQRT 2 and - SQRT 2; in other
   words, the solution set is { SQRT 2, - SQRT 2}.

   When an equation contains several unknowns, and when one has several
   equations with more unknowns than equations, the solution set is often
   infinite. In this case, the solutions cannot be listed. For
   representing them, a [159]parametrization is often useful, which
   consists of expressing the solutions in terms of some of the unknowns
   or auxiliary variables. This is always possible when all the equations
   are [160]linear.

   Such infinite solution sets can naturally be interpreted as
   [161]geometric shapes such as [162]lines, [163]curves (see picture),
   [164]planes, and more generally [165]algebraic varieties or
   [166]manifolds. In particular, [167]algebraic geometry may be viewed as
   the study of solution sets of [168]algebraic equations.

Methods of solution

   [[169]edit]

   The methods for solving equations generally depend on the type of
   equation, both the kind of expressions in the equation and the kind of
   values that may be assumed by the unknowns. The variety in types of
   equations is large, and so are the corresponding methods. Only a few
   specific types are mentioned below.

   In general, given a class of equations, there may be no known
   systematic method ([170]algorithm) that is guaranteed to work. This may
   be due to a lack of mathematical knowledge; some problems were only
   solved after centuries of effort. But this also reflects that, in
   general, no such method can exist: some problems are known to be
   [171]unsolvable by an algorithm, such as [172]Hilbert's tenth problem,
   which was proved unsolvable in 1970.

   For several classes of equations, algorithms have been found for
   solving them, some of which have been implemented and incorporated in
   [173]computer algebra systems, but often require no more sophisticated
   technology than pencil and paper. In some other cases, [174]heuristic
   methods are known that are often successful but that are not guaranteed
   to lead to success.

Brute force, trial and error, inspired guess

   [[175]edit]

   If the solution set of an equation is restricted to a finite set (as is
   the case for equations in [176]modular arithmetic, for example), or can
   be limited to a finite number of possibilities (as is the case with
   some [177]Diophantine equations), the solution set can be found by
   [178]brute force, that is, by testing each of the possible values
   ([179]candidate solutions). It may be the case, though, that the number
   of possibilities to be considered, although finite, is so huge that an
   [180]exhaustive search is not practically feasible; this is, in fact, a
   requirement for strong [181]encryption methods.

   As with all kinds of [182]problem solving, [183]trial and error may
   sometimes yield a solution, in particular where the form of the
   equation, or its similarity to another equation with a known solution,
   may lead to an "inspired guess" at the solution. If a guess, when
   tested, fails to be a solution, consideration of the way in which it
   fails may lead to a modified guess.

Systems of linear equations

   [[186]edit]

   Smaller [187]systems of linear equations can be solved likewise by
   methods of elementary algebra. For solving larger systems, algorithms
   are used that are based on [188]linear algebra. See [189]Gaussian
   elimination and [190]numerical solution of linear systems.

Polynomial equations

   [[191]edit]
   Main article: [192]Solving polynomial equations
   See also: [193]System of polynomial equations

   [194]Polynomial equations of degree up to four can be solved exactly
   using algebraic methods, of which the [195]quadratic formula is the
   simplest example. Polynomial equations with a degree of five or higher
   require in general numerical methods (see below) or special functions
   such as [196]Bring radicals, although some specific cases may be
   solvable algebraically, for example

          4x^{5}-x^{3}-3=0}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
          {\displaystyle 4x^{5}-x^{3}-3=0}

   (by using the [197]rational root theorem), and

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
          displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow

   , which simplifies this to a [198]quadratic equation in z).

Diophantine equations

   [[199]edit]

   In [200]Diophantine equations the solutions are required to be
   [201]integers. In some cases a brute force approach can be used, as
   mentioned above. In some other cases, in particular if the equation is
   in one unknown, it is possible to solve the equation for
   [202]rational-valued unknowns (see [203]Rational root theorem), and
   then find solutions to the Diophantine equation by restricting the
   solution set to integer-valued solutions. For example, the polynomial
   equation

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
          displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mn>2</mn> <msup>
          <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>5</mn> </mrow>
          </msup> <mo>-</mo> <mn>5</mn> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow
          class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>4</mn> </mrow> </msup> <mo>-</mo>
          <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>3</mn>
          </mrow> </msup> <mo>-</mo> <mn>7</mn> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow
          class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>2</mn> </mrow> </msup> <mo>+</mo>
          <mn>2</mn> <mi>x</mi> <mo>+</mo> <mn>3</mn> <mo>=</mo>
          <mn>0</mn> <mspace width="thinmathspace" /> </mstyle> </mrow>
          <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
          2x^{5}-5x^{4}-x^{3}-7x^{2}+2x+3=0\,}</annotation> </semantics>
          :MATH]
          {\displaystyle 2x^{5}-5x^{4}-x^{3}-7x^{2}+2x+3=0\,}

   has as rational solutions x = -&#8288;1/2&#8288;
   and x = 3, and so, viewed as a Diophantine equation, it has the unique
   solution x = 3.

   In general, however, Diophantine equations are among the most difficult
   equations to solve.

Inverse functions

   [[204]edit]
   See also: [205]Inverse problem

   In the simple case of a function of one variable, say, h(x), we can
   solve an equation of the form h(x) = c for some constant c by
   considering what is known as the [206]inverse function of h.

   Given a function h : A -> B, the inverse function, denoted h^-1 and
   defined as h^-1 : B -> A, is a function such that

Factorization

   [[212]edit]

   If the left-hand side expression of an equation P = 0 can be
   [213]factorized as P = QR, the solution set of the original solution
   consists of the union of the solution sets of the two equations Q = 0
   and R = 0. For example, the equation

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

          \tan x+\cot x=2}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
          {\displaystyle \tan x+\cot x=2}

   can be rewritten, using the identity tan x cot x = 1 as

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

          class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mfrac> <mrow> <msup> <mi>tan</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>2</mn> </mrow> </msup>
          <mo>&#x2061;</mo> <mi>x</mi> <mo>-</mo> <mn>2</mn> <mi>tan</mi>

          <mrow> <mi>tan</mi> <mo>&#x2061;</mo> <mi>x</mi> </mrow>
          </mfrac> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <mn>0</mn> <mo>,</mo> </mstyle>
          </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
          {\frac {\tan ^{2}x-2\tan x+1}{\tan x}}=0,}</annotation>
          </semantics> :MATH]
          {\displaystyle {\frac {\tan ^{2}x-2\tan x+1}{\tan x}}=0,}

   which can be factorized into

          [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

          class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mfrac> <msup> <mrow> <mo>(</mo> <mrow>

Numerical methods

   [[214]edit]

   With more complicated equations in real or [215]complex numbers, simple
   methods to solve equations can fail. Often, [216]root-finding
   algorithms like the [217]Newton-Raphson method can be used to find a
   numerical solution to an equation, which, for some applications, can be
   entirely sufficient to solve some problem. There are also
   [218]numerical methods for systems of linear equations.

Matrix equations

   [[219]edit]

   Equations involving [220]matrices and [221]vectors of [222]real numbers
   can often be solved by using methods from [223]linear algebra.

Differential equations

   [[224]edit]

   There is a vast body of methods for solving various kinds of
   [225]differential equations, both [226]numerically and
   [227]analytically. A particular class of problem that can be considered
   to belong here is [228]integration, and the analytic methods for
   solving this kind of problems are now called [229]symbolic
   integration.^[[230]citation needed] Solutions of differential equations
   can be [231]implicit or explicit.^[232][1]
