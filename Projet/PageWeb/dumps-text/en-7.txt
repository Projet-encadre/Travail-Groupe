   Arithmetic operation, inverse of nth power
   This article is about nth-roots of real and complex numbers. For other
   uses, see [143]Root (disambiguation) § Mathematics.

   help [145]improve this article by [146]adding citations to reliable
   sources. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.
   Find sources: [147]"Nth root" - [148]news · [149]newspapers ·

   In [154]mathematics, an nth root of a [155]number x is a number r (the
   root) which, when [156]raised to the power of the [157]positive integer
   n, yields x:
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>r</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo> <munder>
   <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-OP MJX-fixedlimits"> <munder> <mrow>
   <mi>r</mi> <mo>×</mo> <mi>r</mi> <mo>×</mo> <mo>.3</mo> <mo>×</mo>
   <mi>r</mi> </mrow> <mo>&#x23DF;</mo> </munder> </mrow> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mtext> factors</mtext> </mrow> </mrow> </munder> <mo>=</mo> <mi>x</mi>
   <mo>.</mo> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle r^{n}=\underbrace {r\times
   r\times \dotsb \times r} _{n{\text{ factors}}}=x.}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle r^{n}=\underbrace {r\times r\times \dotsb \times r}
   _{n{\text{ factors}}}=x.}

   The integer n is called the index or degree, and the number x of which
   the root is taken is the radicand. A root of degree 2 is called a
   [158]square root and a root of degree 3, a [159]cube root. Roots of
   higher degree are referred by using [160]ordinal numbers, as in fourth
   root, twentieth root, etc. The computation of an nth root is a root
   extraction.

   For example, 3 is a square root of 9, since 3^2 = 9, and -3 is also a
   square root of 9, since (-3)^2 = 9.

   The nth root of x is written as
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} using the [161]radical symbol
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <msqrt> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mphantom> <mi>x</mi>
   </mphantom> </mrow> </msqrt> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\sqrt {\phantom
   {x}}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\phantom {x}}}} . The square root is usually
   written without the n as just &#8288;
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <msqrt> <mi>x</mi> </msqrt> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\sqrt {x}}}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt {x}}} &#8288;. Taking the nth root of a number is
   the [162]inverse operation of [163]exponentiation,^[164][1] and can be
   written as a [165]fractional exponent:

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>1</mn> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mo>/</mo> </mrow> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> <mo>.</mo> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}=x^{1/n}.}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}=x^{1/n}.}

   For a positive real number x,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <msqrt> <mi>x</mi> </msqrt> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\sqrt {x}}}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt {x}}} denotes the positive square root of x and
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} denotes the positive real nth root. A
   negative real number -x has no real-valued square roots, but when x is
   treated as a complex number it has two [166]imaginary square roots,
   &#8288;
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>+</mo> <mi>i</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <msqrt> <mi>x</mi> </msqrt> </mrow> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   +i{\sqrt {x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle +i{\sqrt {x}}} &#8288; and &#8288;
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>-</mo> <mi>i</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <msqrt> <mi>x</mi> </msqrt> </mrow> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   -i{\sqrt {x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle -i{\sqrt {x}}} &#8288;, where i is the [167]imaginary
   unit.

   In general, any non-zero [168]complex number has n distinct
   complex-valued nth roots, equally distributed around a complex circle
   of constant [169]absolute value. (The nth root of 0 is zero with
   [170]multiplicity n, and this circle degenerates to a point.)
   Extracting the nth roots of a complex number x can thus be taken to be
   a [171]multivalued function. By convention the [172]principal value of
   this function, called the principal root and denoted &#8288;
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} &#8288;, is taken to be the nth root
   with the greatest real part and in the special case when x is a
   negative real number, the one with a positive [173]imaginary part. The
   principal root of a positive real number is thus also a positive real
   number. As a [174]function, the principal root is [175]continuous in
   the whole [176]complex plane, except along the negative real axis.

   An unresolved root, especially one using the radical symbol, is
   sometimes referred to as a surd^[177][2] or a radical.^[178][3] Any
   expression containing a radical, whether it is a square root, a cube
   root, or a higher root, is called a radical expression, and if it
   contains no [179]transcendental functions or [180]transcendental
   numbers it is called an [181]algebraic expression.

   Roots are used for determining the [197]radius of convergence of a
   [198]power series with the [199]root test. The nth roots of 1 are
   called [200]roots of unity and play a fundamental role in various areas
   of mathematics, such as [201]number theory, [202]theory of equations,
   and [203]Fourier transform.

History

   [[204]edit]

   Main articles: [205]Square root § History, and [206]Cube root § History

   An archaic term for the operation of taking nth roots is
   radication.^[207][4]^[208][5]

Definition and notation

   [[209]edit]

   [210][220px-NegativeOne4Root.svg.png] The four 4th roots of -1,
   none of which are real[211][220px-NegativeOne3Root.svg.png] The three
   3rd roots of -1,
   one of which is a negative real

   An nth root of a number x, where n is a positive integer, is any of the
   n real or complex numbers r whose nth power is x:

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>r</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo>
   <mi>x</mi> <mo>.</mo> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle r^{n}=x.}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle r^{n}=x.}

   Every positive [212]real number x has a single positive nth root,
   called the [213]principal nth root, which is written
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} . For n equal to 2 this is called the
   principal square root and the n is omitted. The nth root can also be
   represented using [214]exponentiation as x^1/n.

   For even values of n, positive numbers also have a negative nth root,
   while negative numbers do not have a real nth root. For odd values of
   n, every negative number x has a real negative nth root. For example,
   -2 has a real 5th root,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mrow> <mo>-</mo> <mn>2</mn> </mrow> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>5</mn> </mrow> </mroot> </mrow> <mo>=</mo>
   <mo>-</mo> <mn>1.148698354</mn> <mo>...</mo> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{5}]{-2}}=-1.148698354\ldots }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{5}]{-2}}=-1.148698354\ldots } but -2 does not
   have any real 6th roots.

   Every non-zero number x, real or [215]complex, has n different complex
   number nth roots. (In the case x is real, this count includes any real
   nth roots.) The only complex root of 0 is 0.

   The nth roots of almost all numbers (all integers except the nth
   powers, and all rationals except the quotients of two nth powers) are
   [216]irrational. For example,

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <msqrt> <mn>2</mn> </msqrt> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <mn>1.414213562</mn>
   <mo>...</mo> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\sqrt
   {2}}=1.414213562\ldots }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}=1.414213562\ldots }

   All nth roots of rational numbers are [217]algebraic numbers, and all
   nth roots of integers are [218]algebraic integers.

   The term "surd" traces back to [219]Al-Khwarizmi (c. 825), who referred
   to rational and irrational numbers as audible and inaudible,
   respectively. This later led to the Arabic word aHc+m+ (asamm, meaning
   "deaf" or "dumb") for irrational number being translated into Latin as
   surdus (meaning "deaf" or "mute"). [220]Gerard of Cremona (c. 1150),
   [221]Fibonacci (1202), and then [222]Robert Recorde (1551) all used the
   term to refer to unresolved irrational roots, that is, expressions of
   the form
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{r}}} , in which
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>n</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle n}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle n} and
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>r</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle r}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle r} are integer numerals and the whole expression denotes
   an irrational number.^[223][6] Irrational numbers of the form

   A square root of a number x is a number r which, when [229]squared,
   becomes x:

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

   Every positive real number has two square roots, one positive and one
   negative. For example, the two square roots of 25 are 5 and -5. The
   positive square root is also known as the principal square root, and is
   denoted with a radical sign:

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

   Since the square of every real number is nonnegative, negative numbers
   do not have real square roots. However, for every negative real number
   there are two [230]imaginary square roots. For example, the square
   roots of -25 are 5i and -5i, where [231]i represents a number whose
   square is -1.

   [[240]edit]

   x[3] = 2.02439 7458...

   x[4] = 2.02439 74584 99885 04251 08172...

   x[5] = 2.02439 74584 99885 04251 08172 45541 93741 91146 21701 07311

   (All correct digits shown.)

   good for 51.

Digit-by-digit calculation of principal roots of decimal (base 10) numbers

   [[258]edit]

   [259][220px-PascalForDecimalRoots.svg.png] [260]Pascal's triangle
   showing

   Building on the [261]digit-by-digit calculation of a square root, it
   can be seen that the formula used there,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>x</mi> <mo

   triangle. For the nth root of a number
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>P</mi> <mo

   {\displaystyle P(n,i)} is defined as the value of element
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>i</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle i}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle i} in row
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>n</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle n}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle n} of Pascal's Triangle such that
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>P</mi> <mo
   stretchy="false">(</mo> <mn>4</mn> <mo>,</mo> <mn>1</mn> <mo

   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle

   {\displaystyle P(4,1)=4} , we can rewrite the expression as
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <munderover> <mo>\sum</mo> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>i</mi> <mo>=</mo> <mn>0</mn> </mrow> <mrow

   convenience, call the result of this expression
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>y</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle y}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle y} . Using this more general expression, any positive
   principal root can be computed, digit-by-digit, as follows.

   Write the original number in decimal form. The numbers are written
   similar to the [262]long division algorithm, and, as in long division,
   the root will be written on the line above. Now separate the digits
   into groups of digits equating to the root being taken, starting from
   the decimal point and going both left and right. The decimal point of
   the root will be above the decimal point of the radicand. One digit of
   the root will appear above each group of digits of the original number.

   Beginning with the left-most group of digits, do the following
   procedure for each group:
    1. Starting on the left, bring down the most significant (leftmost)
       group of digits not yet used (if all the digits have been used,
       write "0" the number of times required to make a group) and write
       them to the right of the remainder from the previous step (on the
       first step, there will be no remainder). In other words, multiply
       the remainder by
       [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
       displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mn>10</mn> <mrow
       class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mstyle>
       </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
       10^{n}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
       {\displaystyle 10^{n}} and add the digits from the next group. This
       will be the current value c.
    2. Find p and x, as follows:
          + Let
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>p</mi> </mstyle>
            </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle p}</annotation>
            </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle p} be the part of the root found so far,
            ignoring any decimal point. (For the first step,
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>p</mi> <mo>=</mo>
            <mn>0</mn> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle p=0}</annotation>
            </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle p=0} and
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mn>0</mn> <mrow
            class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>0</mn> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo>
            <mn>1</mn> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
            0^{0}=1}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle 0^{0}=1} ).
          + Determine the greatest digit
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>x</mi> </mstyle>
            </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle x}</annotation>
            </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle x} such that
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>y</mi> <mo><=</mo>
            <mi>c</mi> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle y\leq
            c}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle y\leq c} .
          + Place the digit
            [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
            displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>x</mi> </mstyle>
            </mrow> <annotation
            encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle x}</annotation>
            </semantics> :MATH]
            {\displaystyle x} as the next digit of the root, i.e., above
            the group of digits you just brought down. Thus the next p
            will be the old p times 10 plus x.
    3. Subtract
       [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
       displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>y</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
       <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
       y}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
       {\displaystyle y} from
       [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
       displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>c</mi> </mstyle> </mrow>
       <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
       c}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
       {\displaystyle c} to form a new remainder.
    4. If the remainder is zero and there are no more digits to bring
       down, then the algorithm has terminated. Otherwise go back to step
       1 for another iteration.

Logarithmic calculation

   [[267]edit]

   The principal nth root of a positive number can be computed using
   [268]logarithms. Starting from the equation that defines r as an nth
   root of x, namely

   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle r^{n}=x,}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle r^{n}=x,} with x positive and therefore its principal
   root r also positive, one takes logarithms of both sides (any [269]base
   of the logarithm will do) to obtain

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>n</mi> <msub> <mi>log</mi>

   </mrow> <mo>.</mo> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle n\log _{b}r=\log _{b}x\quad

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

   root r which is also negative. This can be found by first multiplying
   both sides of the defining equation by -1 to obtain
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

Geometric constructibility

   [[271]edit]

   The [272]ancient Greek mathematicians knew how to [273]use compass and
   straightedge to construct a length equal to the square root of a given
   length, when an auxiliary line of unit length is given. In 1837
   [274]Pierre Wantzel proved that an nth root of a given length cannot be
   constructed if n is not a power of 2.^[275][11]

   number, come in [294]additive inverse pairs, so that if a number r[1]
   is one of the nth roots then r[2] = -r[1] is another. This is because
   raising the latter's coefficient -1 to the nth power for even n yields
   1: that is, (-r[1])^n = (-1)^n × r[1]^n = r[1]^n.

   As with square roots, the formula above does not define a
   [295]continuous function over the entire complex plane, but instead has
   a [296]branch cut at points where th / n is discontinuous.

Solving polynomials

   [[297]edit]

   See also: [298]Root-finding algorithms

   It was once [299]conjectured that all [300]polynomial equations could
   be [301]solved algebraically (that is, that all roots of a
   [302]polynomial could be expressed in terms of a finite number of
   radicals and [303]elementary operations). However, while this is true
   for third degree polynomials ([304]cubics) and fourth degree
   polynomials ([305]quartics), the [306]Abel-Ruffini theorem (1824) shows
   that this is not true in general when the degree is 5 or greater. For
   example, the solutions of the equation

   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>x</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mn>5</mn> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo>
   <mi>x</mi> <mo>+</mo> <mn>1</mn> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle x^{5}=x+1}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle x^{5}=x+1}

   cannot be expressed in terms of radicals. (cf. [307]quintic equation)

Proof of irrationality for non-perfect nth power x

   [[308]edit]

   Assume that
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} is rational. That is, it can be reduced
   to a fraction
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mfrac> <mi>a</mi> <mi>b</mi> </mfrac> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\frac
   {a}{b}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\frac {a}{b}}} , where a and b are integers without a
   common factor.

   This means that
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>x</mi> <mo>=</mo> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mfrac> <msup> <mi>a</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> <msup> <mi>b</mi>
   <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mfrac>
   </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle x={\frac
   {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle x={\frac {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}} .

   Since x is an integer,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>a</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   a^{n}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle a^{n}} and
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mi>b</mi> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   b^{n}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle b^{n}} must share a common factor if
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>b</mi> <mo>!=</mo> <mn>1</mn>
   </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle b\neq 1}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle b\neq 1} . This means that if
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>b</mi> <mo>!=</mo> <mn>1</mn>
   </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle b\neq 1}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle b\neq 1} ,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mfrac> <msup> <mi>a</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi>
   </mrow> </msup> <msup> <mi>b</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mfrac> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow>
   <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\frac
   {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\frac {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}} is not in simplest form. Thus b
   should equal 1.

   Since
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <msup> <mn>1</mn> <mrow
   class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> <mo>=</mo>
   <mn>1</mn> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle 1^{n}=1}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle 1^{n}=1} and
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mfrac> <mi>n</mi> <mn>1</mn> </mfrac> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <mi>n</mi>
   </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\frac
   {n}{1}}=n}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\frac {n}{1}}=n} ,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mfrac> <msup> <mi>a</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi>
   </mrow> </msup> <msup> <mi>b</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup> </mfrac> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <msup>
   <mi>a</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup>
   </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle {\frac
   {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}=a^{n}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\frac {a^{n}}{b^{n}}}=a^{n}} .

   This means that
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>x</mi> <mo>=</mo> <msup>
   <mi>a</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow> </msup>
   </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle x=a^{n}}</annotation>
   </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle x=a^{n}} and thus,
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> <mo>=</mo> <mi>a</mi> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}=a}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}=a} . This implies that
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} is an integer. Since x is not a perfect
   nth power, this is impossible. Thus
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mi>x</mi> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{x}}} is irrational.
