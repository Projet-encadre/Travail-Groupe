   [109][220px-One5Root.svg.png] The 5th roots of unity (blue points) in
   the [110]complex plane

   In [111]mathematics, a root of unity, occasionally called a [112]de
   Moivre number, is any [113]complex number that yields 1 when
   [114]raised to some positive [115]integer power n. Roots of unity are
   used in many branches of mathematics, and are especially important in
   [116]number theory, the theory of [117]group characters, and the
   [118]discrete Fourier transform.

   Roots of unity can be defined in any [119]field. If the
   [120]characteristic of the field is zero, the roots are complex numbers
   that are also [121]algebraic integers. For fields with a positive
   characteristic, the roots belong to a [122]finite field, and,
   [123]conversely, every nonzero element of a finite field is a root of
   unity. Any [124]algebraically closed field contains exactly n nth roots
   of unity, except when n is a multiple of the (positive) characteristic
   of the field.

   otherwise, belong to a [137]finite field. Conversely, every nonzero
   element in a finite field is a root of unity in that field. See
   [138]Root of unity modulo n and [139]Finite field for further details.

   If n is a [142]prime number, then all nth roots of unity, except 1, are
   primitive.^[143][6]

   In the above formula in terms of exponential and trigonometric
   functions, the primitive nth roots of unity are those for which k and n
   are [144]coprime integers.

   Subsequent sections of this article will comply with complex roots of
   unity. For the case of roots of unity in fields of nonzero
   characteristic, see [145]Finite field § Roots of unity. For the case of
   roots of unity in rings of [146]modular integers, see [147]Root of
   unity modulo n.

   Therefore, given a power z^a of z, one has z^a = z^r, where 0 <= r < n

   Let z be a primitive nth root of unity. Then the powers z, z^2, ...,
   z^n-1, z^n = z^0 = 1 are nth roots of unity and are all distinct. (If
   z^a = z^b where 1 <= a < b <= n, then z^b-a = 1, which would imply that
   z would not be primitive.) This implies that z, z^2, ..., z^n-1, z^n =
   z^0 = 1 are all of the nth roots of unity, since an nth-[155]degree
   [156]polynomial equation over a field (in this case the field of
   complex numbers) has at most n solutions.

Group properties

   [[165]edit]

Group of all roots of unity

   [[166]edit]

   The product and the [167]multiplicative inverse of two roots of unity
   are also roots of unity. In fact, if x^m = 1 and y^n = 1, then (x^-1)^m
   = 1, and (xy)^k = 1, where k is the [168]least common multiple of m and
   n.

   Therefore, the roots of unity form an [169]abelian group under
   multiplication. This [170]group is the [171]torsion subgroup of the
   [172]circle group.

Group of nth roots of unity

   [[173]edit]

   For an integer n, the product and the multiplicative inverse of two nth
   roots of unity are also nth roots of unity. Therefore, the nth roots of
   unity form an abelian group under multiplication.

   Given a primitive nth root of unity w, the other nth roots are powers
   of w. This means that the group of the nth roots of unity is a
   [174]cyclic group. It is worth remarking that the term of cyclic group
   originated from the fact that this group is a [175]subgroup of the
   [176]circle group.

Algebraic expression

   [[209]edit]

   The nth roots of unity are, by definition, the [210]roots of the
   [211]polynomial x^n - 1, and are thus [212]algebraic numbers. As this
   polynomial is not [213]irreducible (except for n = 1), the primitive
   nth roots of unity are roots of an irreducible polynomial (over the
   integers) of lower degree, called the nth [214]cyclotomic polynomial,
   and often denoted F[n]. The degree of F[n] is given by [215]Euler's
   totient function, which counts (among other things) the number of
   primitive nth roots of unity.^[216][9] The roots of F[n] are exactly
   the primitive nth roots of unity.

   [217]Galois theory can be used to show that the cyclotomic polynomials
   may be conveniently solved in terms of radicals. (The trivial form
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
   <mroot> <mn>1</mn> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mi>n</mi> </mrow>
   </mroot> </mrow> </mstyle> </mrow> <annotation
   encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   {\sqrt[{n}]{1}}}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{n}]{1}}} is not convenient, because it contains
   non-primitive roots, such as 1, which are not roots of the cyclotomic
   polynomial, and because it does not give the real and imaginary parts
   separately.) This means that, for each positive integer n, there exists
   an expression built from integers by root extractions, additions,
   subtractions, multiplications, and divisions (and nothing else), such
   that the primitive nth roots of unity are exactly the set of values
   that can be obtained by choosing values for the root extractions (k
   possible values for a kth root). (For more details see
   [218]§ Cyclotomic fields, below.)

   Gauss [219]proved that a primitive nth root of unity can be expressed
   using only [220]square roots, addition, subtraction, multiplication and
   division if and only if it is possible to [221]construct with compass
   and straightedge the [222]regular n-gon. This is the case [223]if and
   only if n is either a [224]power of two or the product of a power of
   two and [225]Fermat primes that are all different.

   terms of radicals involves nonreal radicals.

Periodicity

   [[239]edit]

   If z is a primitive nth root of unity, then the sequence of powers

          ... , z^-1, z^0, z^1, ...

   is n-periodic (because z^ j + n = z^ jz^ n = z^ j for all values of j),
   and the n sequences of powers

          s[k]: ... , z^ k · (-1), z^ k · 0, z^ k · 1, ...

   for k = 1, ... , n are all n-periodic (because z^ k · (j + n) = z^ k ·
   j). Furthermore, the set {s[1], ... , s[n]} of these sequences is a
   [240]basis of the [241]linear space of all n-periodic sequences. This
   means that any n-periodic sequence of complex numbers

          ... , x[-1] , x[0] , x[1], ...

   can be expressed as a [242]linear combination of powers of a primitive
   nth root of unity:

   SR(n) = 0.

   [[256]edit]

   [263]unitary. That is,

   {\displaystyle \Phi _{p}(z)={\frac {z^{p}-1}{z-1}}=\sum
   _{k=0}^{p-1}z^{k}.} Substituting any positive integer >= 2 for z, this
   sum becomes a [284]base z [285]repunit. Thus a necessary (but not
   sufficient) condition for a repunit to be prime is that its length be
   prime.

   Note that, contrary to first appearances, not all coefficients of all
   cyclotomic polynomials are 0, 1, or -1. The first exception is
   F[[286]105]. It is not a surprise it takes this long to get an example,
   because the behavior of the coefficients depends not so much on n as on
   how many [287]odd prime factors appear in n. More precisely, it can be
   shown that if n has 1 or 2 odd prime factors (for example, n = 150)
   then the nth cyclotomic polynomial only has coefficients 0, 1 or -1.
   Thus the first conceivable n for which there could be a coefficient
   besides 0, 1, or -1 is a product of the three smallest odd primes, and
   that is 3  ·  5  ·  7 = 105. This by itself doesn't prove the 105th
   polynomial has another coefficient, but does show it is the first one
   which even has a chance of working (and then a computation of the
   coefficients shows it does). A theorem of Schur says that there are
   cyclotomic polynomials with coefficients arbitrarily large in
   [288]absolute value. In particular, if

   p_{t},}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]

   Many restrictions are known about the values that cyclotomic
   polynomials can assume at integer values. For example, if p is prime,
   then d £ F[p](d) if and only if d =3 1 (mod p).

   Cyclotomic polynomials are solvable in [290]radicals, as roots of unity
   are themselves radicals. Moreover, there exist more informative radical
   expressions for nth roots of unity with the additional
   property^[291][12] that every value of the expression obtained by
   choosing values of the radicals (for example, signs of square roots) is
   a primitive nth root of unity. This was already shown by [292]Gauss in
   1797.^[293][13] Efficient [294]algorithms exist for calculating such
   expressions.^[295][14]

Cyclic groups

   [[296]edit]

   The nth roots of unity form under multiplication a [297]cyclic group of
   [298]order n, and in fact these groups comprise all of the [299]finite
   subgroups of the [300]multiplicative group of the complex number field.
   A [301]generator for this cyclic group is a primitive nth root of
   unity.

   The nth roots of unity form an irreducible [302]representation of any
   cyclic group of order n. The orthogonality relationship also follows
   from [303]group-theoretic principles as described in [304]Character
   group.

   The roots of unity appear as entries of the [305]eigenvectors of any
   [306]circulant matrix; that is, matrices that are invariant under
   cyclic shifts, a fact that also follows from [307]group representation
   theory as a variant of [308]Bloch's
   theorem.^[309][15]^[[310]page needed] In particular, if a circulant
   [311]Hermitian matrix is considered (for example, a discretized
   one-dimensional [312]Laplacian with periodic boundaries^[313][16]), the
   orthogonality property immediately follows from the usual orthogonality
   of eigenvectors of Hermitian matrices.

   nth root of unity may be expressed in term of k-roots, with various k

   [331]Kronecker, usually called the [332]Kronecker-Weber theorem on the
   grounds that Weber completed the proof.

Relation to quadratic integers

   [[333]edit]
   [334][220px-Roots_of_unity%2C_golden_ratio.svg.png] In the [335]complex
   plane, the red points are the fifth roots of unity, and the black
   points are the sums of a fifth root of unity and its complex
   conjugate.[336][160px-Star_polygon_8-2.svg.png] In the complex plane,
   the corners of the two squares are the eighth roots of unity

   For n = 1, 2, both roots of unity [337]1 and [338]-1 are [339]integers.

   For three values of n, the roots of unity are [340]quadratic integers:
     * For n = 3, 6 they are [341]Eisenstein integers ([342]D = -3).
     * For n = 4 they are [343]Gaussian integers (D = -1): see
       [344]Imaginary unit.

   For four other values of n, the primitive roots of unity are not
   quadratic integers, but the sum of any root of unity with its
   [345]complex conjugate (also an nth root of unity) is a quadratic
   integer.

   For n = 5, 10, none of the non-real roots of unity (which satisfy a
   [346]quartic equation) is a quadratic integer, but the sum z + z = 2
   [347]Re z of each root with its complex conjugate (also a 5th root of
   unity) is an element of the [348]ring [349]Z[&#8288;1 +  SQRT
   5/2&#8288;] (D = 5). For two pairs of non-real 5th roots of unity these
   sums are [350]inverse [351]golden ratio and [352]minus golden ratio.

   For n = 8, for any root of unity z + z equals to either 0, ±2, or
   ±[353] SQRT 2 (D = 2).

   For n = 12, for any root of unity, z + z equals to either 0, ±1, ±2 or
   ±[354] SQRT 3 (D = 3).
