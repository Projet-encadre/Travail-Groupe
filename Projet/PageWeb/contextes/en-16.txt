   [109][220px-One5Root.svg.png] The 5th roots of unity (blue points) in
   the [110]complex plane

   In [111]mathematics, a root of unity, occasionally called a [112]de
   Moivre number, is any [113]complex number that yields 1 when
   [114]raised to some positive [115]integer power n. Roots of unity are
   used in many branches of mathematics, and are especially important in
   [116]number theory, the theory of [117]group characters, and the
   [118]discrete Fourier transform.

   Roots of unity can be defined in any [119]field. If the
   [120]characteristic of the field is zero, the roots are complex numbers
   that are also [121]algebraic integers. For fields with a positive
   characteristic, the roots belong to a [122]finite field, and,
   [123]conversely, every nonzero element of a finite field is a root of
   unity. Any [124]algebraically closed field contains exactly n nth roots
   of unity, except when n is a multiple of the (positive) characteristic
   of the field.

Group of all roots of unity

   [[166]edit]

   The product and the [167]multiplicative inverse of two roots of unity
   are also roots of unity. In fact, if x^m = 1 and y^n = 1, then (x^-1)^m
   = 1, and (xy)^k = 1, where k is the [168]least common multiple of m and
   n.

   Therefore, the roots of unity form an [169]abelian group under
   multiplication. This [170]group is the [171]torsion subgroup of the
   [172]circle group.

Group of nth roots of unity

   [[173]edit]

   For an integer n, the product and the multiplicative inverse of two nth
   roots of unity are also nth roots of unity. Therefore, the nth roots of
   unity form an abelian group under multiplication.

   Given a primitive nth root of unity w, the other nth roots are powers
   of w. This means that the group of the nth roots of unity is a
   [174]cyclic group. It is worth remarking that the term of cyclic group
   originated from the fact that this group is a [175]subgroup of the
   [176]circle group.

   The nth roots of unity are, by definition, the [210]roots of the
   [211]polynomial x^n - 1, and are thus [212]algebraic numbers. As this
   polynomial is not [213]irreducible (except for n = 1), the primitive
   nth roots of unity are roots of an irreducible polynomial (over the
   integers) of lower degree, called the nth [214]cyclotomic polynomial,
   and often denoted F[n]. The degree of F[n] is given by [215]Euler's
   totient function, which counts (among other things) the number of
   primitive nth roots of unity.^[216][9] The roots of F[n] are exactly
   the primitive nth roots of unity.

   [334][220px-Roots_of_unity%2C_golden_ratio.svg.png] In the [335]complex
   plane, the red points are the fifth roots of unity, and the black
   points are the sums of a fifth root of unity and its complex

   For n = 1, 2, both roots of unity [337]1 and [338]-1 are [339]integers.

   For three values of n, the roots of unity are [340]quadratic integers:
     * For n = 3, 6 they are [341]Eisenstein integers ([342]D = -3).
     * For n = 4 they are [343]Gaussian integers (D = -1): see
       [344]Imaginary unit.

   For four other values of n, the primitive roots of unity are not
   quadratic integers, but the sum of any root of unity with its
   [345]complex conjugate (also an nth root of unity) is a quadratic
   integer.

   For n = 5, 10, none of the non-real roots of unity (which satisfy a
   [346]quartic equation) is a quadratic integer, but the sum z + z = 2
   [347]Re z of each root with its complex conjugate (also a 5th root of
   unity) is an element of the [348]ring [349]Z[&#8288;1 +  SQRT
   5/2&#8288;] (D = 5). For two pairs of non-real 5th roots of unity these
   sums are [350]inverse [351]golden ratio and [352]minus golden ratio.

   For n = 8, for any root of unity z + z equals to either 0, ±2, or
   ±[353] SQRT 2 (D = 2).

   For n = 12, for any root of unity, z + z equals to either 0, ±1, ±2 or
   ±[354] SQRT 3 (D = 3).
