In the [85]music theory of [86]harmony, the root is a specific [87]note
that names and typifies a given [88]chord. Chords are often spokenroot
about in terms of their root, their [89]quality, and their
[90]extensions. When a chord is named without reference to quality, itroot
is assumed to be [91]major--for example, a "C chord" refers to a Croot
major triad, containing the notes C, E, and G. In a given harmonicroot
context, the root of a chord need not be in [92]the bass position, as
chords may be [93]inverted while retaining the same name, and thereforeroot
the same root.
root
In [94]tertian harmonic theory, wherein chords can be considered stacksroot
of third intervals (e.g. in [95]common practice [96]tonality), the root
of a chord is the [97]note on which the subsequent thirds are stacked.root
For instance, the root of a [98]triad such as E Minor is E,
independently of the vertical order in which the three notes (E, G androot
B) are presented. A triad can be in three possible positions, a "root
position" with the root in the bass (i.e., with the
note, thus E, G, B or E, B, G from lowest to highest notes), aroot
[99]first inversion, e.g. G, B, E or G, E, B (i.e., with the note whichroot
is a third interval above the root, G, as the lowest note) and a
[100]second inversion, e.g. B, E, G or B, G, E, in which the note thatroot
is a fifth interval above the root (B) is the lowest note.
root
Regardless of whether a chord is in root position or in an inversion,
the root remains the same in all three cases. Four-note seventh chords
have four possible positions. That is, the chord can be played with theroot
root as the bass note, the note a third above the
(first inversion), the note a fifth above the root as the bass note
(second inversion), or the note a seventh above the root as the bass
note (third inversion). Five-note ninth chords know five positions,root
six-note eleventh chords know six positions, etc., but the root
position always is that of the stack of thirds, and the root is the
lowest note of this stack (see also [101]Factor (chord)).root

The fundamental bass (basse fondamentale) is a concept proposed byroot
Jean-Philippe Rameau, derived from the thoroughbass, to notate whatroot
would today be called the progression of chord roots rather than the
actual lowest note found in the music, the bassline. From this Rameauroot
formed rules for the progression of chords based on the intervalsroot
between their roots. Subsequently, [196]music theory has typically
treated chordal roots as the defining feature of harmony.^[197][14]

permit chance. The root progression supports the work. The total

another melody, and it determines the tonal basis of the music. Androot
